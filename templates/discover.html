Applications

Satellite imagery, or remote sensing data, provides essential observation and analysis related to the Earth's surface and environment due to their priviledge point of view. See some common applications below.

Meteorology

Weather prediction

Combined with visible light imaging, satellites can capture a nearly full picture of weather systems

Natural Disasters

Combining information about heat and reflected light allows for analysis of cloud systems, pollution, fires, storms, surface temperatures, and more.

Global Warming

Thanks to satellite technology, climate researchers now have more evidence that our planet has experienced abnormal warmth for at least two decades.

Environmental

Deforestation

Scientists have used satellites to track the deforestation of the Amazon rainforest for several decades â€” enough time to see some remarkable shifts in the pace and location of clearing.

Wildfire detection

Satellite imagery can track the spread and destruction of wildfires and smoke.

Drought detection

Satellite imagery can be used to help identify areas of drought over time. Near real-time monitoring of drought conditions can be useful for monitoring crops and forecasting yield.

Urban Changes

Infrastructure Growth

Satellite imagery can captures the changes in infrastructure on Earth, whether referring to city growth over decades, or a smaller-scale urban developement project over a few months

Light pollution

Earth orbiting satellites can intercept light scattered in cities and provide a useful insight to growth rate of cities and electricity usage.

City planning

There's a growing demand for up-to-date land topography and land use maps, as well as other spatial data products. These maps are essential to land use development and engineering applications.

Examples

Read some of our examples of previous clients who have used satellite imagery collected by this platform.

Changes in forestry landscape

Adam owns a mountain bike shop in Dunkeld, Scotland and is heavily involved in local trail maintenance. The mountain bike industry is growing rapidly in the small community, and officials must respond to the demand for new trails, and keeping current trails safe.

Adam uses our platform to search for images of the surrounding hills and forestry areas, and the trail maintenance committee can respond accordingly. Using satellite imagery reduces the hours committee members are sent onto the hills to walk many kilometers of trails in person. The images can show detail of windfall, deforestation, broken features and scope for new trails.

A False Colour Infrared image type is used for this pipeline, as it is ideal for tracking vegetation health or changes. An interval of one month (i.e. one image per month) is suitable for this kind of pipeline, as vegetation changes are very gradual.

Monitoring global ice reduction

Since satellites became equipped to measure sea level, sea surface temperatures, sea-ice fluctuation and vegetation coverage, they have been instrumental in providing environmental scientists with information about the rise in sea temperatures and reduction of polar ice.

Changes in the landscape can be detected either via comparing images taken with extended periods of time between the image capture time, or via the difference in measured light reflection. Snow and ice have a high light reflection index, meaning infrared images taken of the specified region will show differences if the ice level has reduced.

This provides insight into our understanding of ice reduction and overall global warming of the planet.

Glossary

Here is a glossary of technical terms you may find while using this website

AOI The AOI, or Area of Interest, is the determined location on the surface of the Earth, of which you hope to receive satellite images.
AOI coverage The AOI coverage refers to the percentage of the AOI which was selected which could be captured by satellite imaging technology. If a satellite does not have 100% field of vision of the selected AOI, only a portion of that AOI can be captured.
All pipelines created on our platform gurantee that all images will have captured at least 50% of the AOI selected.
API An API is an Application Programming Interface. It is a third-party set of functions which can be 'called' by another platform to provide data. Spaceport uses the Skywatch API to match up your parameters to suitable satellites and create your pipelines.
Archived imagery Imagery which has already been captured and downloaded by satellites. If your pipeline involves any intervals which started and finished before today, it will deliver archived images. These can be delivered straight away, once we have processed them.
Cloud cover percentage This parameter refers to the maximum allowable percentage of the delivered image which can be obscured by clouds.
Field of vision The field of vision (FOV) or field of view is the area on Earth which the satellite has visibility of at a given time.
geoJSON The AOI is represented in geoJSON format, which is a format for encoding a variety of geographic data structures. Each point you choose on the map will have a longitude and latitude, and the geoJSON is compiled of all the coordinates in one array.
Image resolution This refers to the detail an image holds, and how many visible pixels proportionate to the size of the image. An image with a resolution of 10 metres means that each pixel stands for a 10 m x 10 m area on the ground.
Images available to the users on this platform will have a resolution between 8 and 15 metres.
Metadata Metadata is considered 'data about data'; in this case, refers to the raw data about the satellite used to capture the image.
Any images delivered to your pipeline will provide a relevant link with all the metadata for that image. This data is used when post processing the image for whatever the purpose may be.
Output type There are many different kinds of images satellites can capture, for all kinds of purposes.
For example, infrared satellite imagery can be used for global warming projects, to capture temperature differences on the Earth's surface and in the atmosphere.
The output type is the type of image you wish your pipeline to deliver. On this platform, there are six output image types to choose from.
Pipeline A pipeline is a term used across many industries to describe an organised plan, with a start and end date, involving a series of stages.
In this context, we are looking for satellite images between two dates, hopefully delivering results within specified intervals.
It is similar to a project timeline, with defined goals along the way to be met by specific dates.
Satellite aquisition The API used on this platform uses the AOI, interval dates and other parameters selected to match up to the most suitable satellite.
Satellites follow a specific trajectory around the Earth, therefore the API cross references the selected AOI with any satellites orbiting above that AOI. Satellites also have a limited footprint (area of the Earth's surface which is visible to them).
If the satellite orbits above the AOI within the prescribed interval, and can meet all other parameter requirements, this is successful satellite aquisition.
See these sources for more information on satellite trajectories and see satellites in orbit. Esri | Spectator Earth | NY20
Tasking Tasking refers to the future plan of accessing satellite images, in effect intervals which are scheduled for any time after today. Your pipeline can be a mix of archived imagery (past) and tasking intervals (future).
Tasking interval The interval is the length of time between two dates. There will be several intervals within the pipeline. If the interval is set to one day, the user wishes to capture an image of that AOI every day. The longer the interval, the higher chance an image can be found for that interval, as there is less restriction on when the image can be taken and by which satellite.

FAQ

Here are some frequently asked questions about our platform.

How can I receive images straight away?

If you'd like to start collecting images immediately, you can set up your pipeline to look for archived imagery - images from the past. The platform will deliver these as soon as they are gathered and processed.

My pipeline starts and finishes in the past, why is it still 'active'?

Satellite imagery retrieval can take a few minutes. If your pipeline is still active, it is likely still either retrieving, processing or formatting the images. You are recommended to allow a few minutes for this process, and welcome to update the pipeline until complete.

Why are some of my images taken in the day, and some at night?

As long as the images have been captured within the specified interval, they will be delivered. If the interval is set to 1 day, the image could have been captured at any time within those 24 hours, day and night. Intervals cannot be set to more specific times, such as within the hour or minute.

Why was my pipeline unsuccessful in delivering images?

Successful retrieval of images depends on many factors. Most importantly, in order to capture an image of the area selected (AOI), a satellite must be scheduled to have the area in their field of vision within the specified interval dates. We do not have 100% satellite coverage of Earth, therefore it is entirely possible for the AOI to not have coverage by any satellites within the interval timeframe. We recommend increasing the interval period and maximising the AOI for a higher probability of satellite aquisition, and therefore delivered results.

What can I do with my satellite images?

Spaceport is a website designed to provoke curiosity in those unfamiliar with satellite imagery. If you have an interest in using the images for a specific purpose, you can:

  • - compare all images from the same pipeline, to distinguish changes in the environment
  • - browse the metadata for technical information about the images
  • - download the images and apply standard image processing techniques, such as increasing or decreasing the exposure, contrast, saturation, and hue, to determine if you can enhance the image
  • - satellite imagery is cool - share them with your friends!

Which satellites does the platform use to collect images?

Spaceport has access to the Sentinel-2 (ESA), PlanetScope (Planet), SPOT (Airbus), Skysat (Planet), Kompsat-3 (SIIS), and Pleiades (Airbus) satellite data.

Why are my images blurry when I zoom in?

In order to offer free satellite imagery, Spaceport can only access 'low resolution' data, representing an image resolution of 8 - 15 metres. A resolution of 8 metres means that each pixel on the image represents an area on Earth of 8 metres squared. If the image has captured an area of 5,000 km2, that is a very large area on Earth to capture in an image which is a size that could be rendered on a computer screen, so we have to sacrifice some image quality. The images can still be useful and interesting to look at, and higher resolution images can be purchased on other websites.
